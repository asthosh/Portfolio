    Personal Growth Stories

    I was given a chance to speak at the launch event of Muthoot Business School. It was a proud moment for me because the event had many important people like MP, industry heads, board members, and academic leaders. I had prepared my speech well, but when it was time to speak, I got very nervous. I forgot some lines and didn't speak the way I had practiced. I felt like I messed it up.

    After the speech, I felt really disappointed and a little embarrassed. My hands were shaking and my voice wasn't steady. I felt bad because I knew people had trusted me to do well. At the same time, I was thankful for the opportunity. It showed that others believed I could do it, even if I didn't perform my best.

    This wasn't the first time I've felt nervous before speaking in public. I've experienced stage fear during a school event too. But I usually managed to get through it. This time, it reminded me that I still need to work on building my confidence, especially when speaking in front of large or important audiences.

    I realised that I became nervous because I was overthinking the audience and trying too hard to be perfect. Instead of focusing on just delivering my message, I kept thinking about how I would be judged. This made me lose my focus.

    I now understand that public speaking gets better with practice, and it's okay if things don't go perfectly every time. From now on, I want to take more chances to speak in public even if it's just in front of small groups. I'll try to join activities or clubs where I can practice more. I've learned that being real and staying calm is better than trying to give a perfect speech. Next time, I'll focus more on connecting with the audience than memorizing every word.

    During my first internship review, I shared that I had started learning how to multitask and was using a priority matrix to sort tasks based on importance and urgency. My manager responded by saying, "everything is important". This made me pause, because I felt it's not realistic to try doing ten things at once.

    At that moment, I kept quiet, but in my mind, I was thinking, "If everything is important, how do we actually decide what to do first?" I felt a bit frustrated, as it seemed like my approach to prioritizing work was being dismissed. But after reflecting on it later, I understood that my manager was trying to explain that in a work environment, every task has its own value and impact.

    I've faced similar situations before during college, where I had to handle multiple assignments and deadlines. I always believed in focusing on the most urgent and important first. This feedback reminded me that in a professional setting, it's also about finding ways to give attention to all tasks without ignoring any.

    I realised my frustration came from taking "multitasking" too literally. My manager probably meant that while prioritizing is important, I should also be adaptable enough to handle different responsibilities as needed. It's more about shifting focus efficiently than trying to do everything at the same time.

    Going forward, I plan to keep using my priority matrix but also work on balancing attention across all tasks. I want to get better at switching between different kinds of work without losing focus or quality, so I can meet deadlines while still valuing each responsibility equally.

    In today's session, I realized an important aspect of my emotional intelligence. I usually have a tendency to take control in group discussions, but this time I consciously chose to step back, remain quiet for a while, and listen to what others had to say. At first, this felt uncomfortable because my instinct was to direct the conversation. However, as I listened, I began to appreciate the range of perspectives that emerged.

    I noticed how much more engaged the group became when I did not immediately take charge, and I felt satisfied seeing the discussion become richer. This reminded me of earlier experiences in group tasks where I often dominated discussions, thinking that being the first to lead was the best way to move forward.

    Connecting today's experience with those moments made me realize that my strength lies in self-awareness, the ability to recognize my behavior and its impact on the group. Reflecting further, I understood that awareness alone is not enough. According to Daniel Goleman's framework, self-awareness must be paired with self-regulation to be effective.

    Here I see my gap. Even when I notice my controlling instinct, I do not always manage it well, especially in high-pressure situations. This limits collaboration and sometimes makes decisions less inclusive, as they tend to reflect my view more than the team's.

    Going forward, I want to address this gap with two deliberate actions. First, I will practice active listening by summarizing or acknowledging what others share before adding my own input. Second, I will follow a simple rule of listening to at least two people before I speak in group discussions.

    These small but concrete steps will help me regulate my instinct to control and create space for others to contribute. Through this reflection, I have come to see that while self-awareness gives me the chance to notice my behavior, self-regulation is what will help me grow into a leader who listens first, empowers others, and builds stronger teamwork.

    I couldn't attend the session, but I spoke to a few classmates and went through the PPT to understand what was taught. The discussion was mainly about Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs, Herzberg's Two-Factor Theory and Vroom's Expectancy Theory. Out of these, Vroom's theory made the most sense to me personally. The idea that motivation comes from the belief that our efforts will lead to results, and that those results will lead to something meaningful, really reflects how I think and feel.

    There was a situation I remember very clearly where I gave everything I had. I showed up every single day, worked hard, stayed consistent, and genuinely believed my effort would be noticed. But when it came to recognition, it went to someone who hadn't been equally involved. I wasn't upset that I didn't get it, but it felt unfair. If it had gone to someone who truly deserved it, I would've felt okay. But watching it go to someone who didn't put in the same effort was honestly demotivating. It made me question whether hard work always gets rewarded, and I could feel my motivation drop.

    On the other hand, there was my college project. I had set a clear goal of publishing a paper in IEEE. I worked with complete focus for months, and when it finally got published, I felt so proud. That moment felt like proof that my effort and persistence were worth it. It made me want to keep pushing myself even more.

    When I look at both these situations, I can clearly see the difference in how I felt. In the first one, when my effort didn't match the outcome, my motivation crashed. But in the second, when it did, it gave me a huge boost. That's exactly what Vroom's Expectancy Theory talks about - the link between effort, performance and reward.

    I also see how Herzberg's Two-Factor Theory fits in, because recognition is a big motivator for me. Going forward, as a future manager, I really want to make sure people feel their work is valued and seen. For myself, I need to keep setting goals that matter to me but also learn to stay strong when things don't go the way I expect. And for my team, I'd focus on giving fair recognition and creating clarity on how their efforts contribute to the bigger picture. I think that would make a real difference in keeping people motivated.
